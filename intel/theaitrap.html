Executive Summary

The rapid proliferation of subsidized Artificial Intelligence (AI) models is creating a deceptive landscape within the software ecosystem. While appearing to democratize access and accelerate innovation, this artificial pricing structure poses significant risks, potentially leading to a market bubble and a fundamental reshaping of industry power dynamics.

Startups heavily reliant on subsidized AI face precarious futures, while established players must strategically navigate this volatile environment. This white paper examines the potential consequences of this subsidized AI dependency and proposes critical considerations for businesses as the market inevitably recalibrates.

This "subsidy trap" creates an environment where smaller AI startups struggle to compete despite significant venture funding, ultimately setting the stage for industry consolidation where big tech companies can acquire these startups or force them out of business once subsidies are withdrawn.

AI services present a striking economic paradox in 2025 — while consumer-facing prices continue to drop dramatically, the underlying costs to build and operate these systems remain extraordinarily high. This disconnect forms the foundation of what we call "The Great Subsidy Trap."

The True Infrastructure Costs

The backbone of modern AI systems consists of specialized computing infrastructure that carries enormous costs:

  • Data Center Construction: AI-optimized data centers cost approximately $25 million per megawatt of capacity, with large-scale facilities requiring gigawatts of power.
  • Hardware: High-end AI chips like NVIDIA's H200 and Blackwell series remain in constrained supply and command premium prices.
  • Energy Consumption: By 2027, AI training servers will collectively consume between 85-134 Terawatt-hours annually — comparable to the electricity usage of entire countries like Sweden or the Netherlands.
  • Specialized Talent: Senior ML engineers command salaries of $200,000+, with overheads potentially matching 50-100% of base compensation.

"We're witnessing perhaps the most heavily subsidized technology rollout in history. The gap between what AI actually costs to build and run versus what customers are paying is creating an artificial market that cannot be sustained indefinitely."

— Casey Potenzone, Founder, NoFriction

OpenAI: A Case Study in Unsustainable Economics

OpenAI presents the most striking example of the economic disconnect:

  • $13 billion on compute with Microsoft alone in 2025
  • $12.9 billion five-year compute deal with CoreWeave (~$2.38 billion annually)
  • $19 billion commitment to the Stargate data center project
  • Total 2025 costs: $28 billion+ with projected revenue of $11 billion
  • Projected 2025 losses: $14+ billion while growing its customer base

2. The Strategic "Subsidy Trap" and Market Division

The massive disconnect between AI costs and pricing is not accidental — it's a deliberate strategy employed by well-capitalized tech giants to gain market dominance. This "Subsidy Trap" involves three key strategic phases:

Phase 1: Subsidize

Heavily subsidize AI services to accelerate adoption, establish dependency, and expand market share while operating at significant losses.

Phase 2: Dominate

Use network effects and increasing dependency to solidify market position while smaller competitors struggle to match subsidized pricing.

Phase 3: Consolidate

Acquire struggling competitors at favorable valuations and/or raise prices once sufficient market control is established.

Historical Parallels: Predatory Pricing in the Digital Age

This strategy bears significant resemblance to historical examples of predatory pricing — selling products below cost to eliminate competition — but with a modern twist. Rather than targeting a single competitor, Big Tech's AI subsidies create market-wide conditions where only the most heavily capitalized players can survive.

"While venture capital has historically subsidized startups until they reach profitability, we now have a scenario where the largest tech companies in the world are subsidizing their own money-losing AI divisions to gain market share. This creates a fundamentally different competitive dynamic that few startups can withstand."

— Leading Venture Capitalist, Quoted in The Information, March 2025

The Subsidy Arms Race

Major tech companies are currently engaged in an unprecedented subsidy "arms race" — each committing extraordinary sums to AI infrastructure in 2025:

Fig 3: Projected AI Infrastructure Investment by Major Tech Companies (2025)

This level of spending is orders of magnitude beyond what even well-funded startups can match, creating an inherently uneven competitive landscape. For context, the $320+ billion in projected 2025 spending by these four companies exceeds the combined value of all AI venture capital investments from 2015-2023.

Customer Acquisition Strategy

The subsidization strategy functions primarily as a massive customer acquisition play. By offering AI capabilities at artificially low prices, companies like OpenAI, Google, and Meta can:

  • Accelerate Adoption: Overcome initial hesitancy through "too good to pass up" pricing
  • Create Technical Dependencies: Integrate AI into core workflows, making switching costs increasingly prohibitive
  • Establish Data Advantages: Each customer interaction provides valuable training data, creating a flywheel effect
  • Suppress Competition: Force smaller providers to match unsustainable pricing or lose market relevance

"By positioning its consumer segment as an acquisition channel, OpenAI can unlock greater value in the enterprise space, where high retention (evidencing the stickiness of a company's product) and recurring usage create more stable and predictable revenue."

— Raphaëlle d'Ornano, "A Look at OpenAI's Economics," 2024

3. Key Players Analysis

The AI market is increasingly dominated by a small number of players with the resources to sustain massive losses while building market share. Each has adopted different strategies within the broader subsidization framework.

Foundation Model Providers

OpenAI

Valuation: $300 billion | Strategy: Consumer-to-Enterprise Funnel

Subsidy Approach: Offering increasingly powerful technology at decreasing prices despite soaring costs. Operating at significant losses ($14+ billion projected for 2025).

Key Investment: $28 billion in infrastructure spending for 2025 alone, including massive deals with Microsoft Azure and CoreWeave.

Strategic Position: Using ChatGPT's vast consumer base as an acquisition channel for enterprise customers. Dependent on investor subsidies and Microsoft's infrastructure support.

Anthropic

Backing: Amazon, Google | Strategy: Enterprise-First Focus

Subsidy Approach: Heavily subsidized by strategic investments from Amazon ($4 billion) and Google ($2 billion), allowing it to operate at significant losses.

Key Investment: Advanced capabilities in safety and reasoning with Claude 3.5 models, focusing on higher-value enterprise applications.

Strategic Position: Positioned as the "safe" alternative to OpenAI, particularly for enterprises with concerns about data privacy and model control.

Big Tech Platforms

Microsoft

Strategy: Embed AI throughout product ecosystem while controlling access to critical infrastructure

Subsidy Approach: Investment of $13 billion in OpenAI while integrating AI capabilities into Office, GitHub, and Windows at minimal additional cost to customers.

Strategic Position: Controls both platform (Azure) and applications (Microsoft 365), creating dependency across the entire AI stack.

Google

Strategy: Defend search dominance while expanding cloud AI offerings

Subsidy Approach: Spending $75 billion on AI in 2025, much focused on Gemini models with multimodal capabilities. Offering search users AI features for free.

Strategic Position: Embedding advanced AI into search to prevent disruption, while simultaneously expanding Google Cloud's AI tools for enterprises.

Meta

Strategy: Open-source approach with Llama models while investing heavily in infrastructure

Subsidy Approach: Planning $60-65 billion in AI infrastructure investment for 2025, including new data centers and massive GPU purchases.

Strategic Position: Using Llama's open-source strategy to gain developer mindshare while focusing monetization on improved advertising performance through AI.

Specialized Players

X (formerly Twitter)

Strategy: Real-time data integration with Grok AI

Subsidy Approach: Including Grok AI capabilities with X Premium+ subscriptions, providing advanced AI features at a consumer-friendly price point.

Strategic Position: Leveraging unique access to X's real-time data to differentiate Grok from other AI models in real-time information access.

NVIDIA

Strategy: Dominate the AI hardware infrastructure layer

Subsidy Approach: Not subsidizing - actually one of the few companies profiting significantly from AI.

Strategic Position: Essential provider of GPU infrastructure, capturing outsized value from the AI boom regardless of which software players ultimately win.

CoreWeave

Strategy: Specialized AI infrastructure provider

Subsidy Approach: Taking on substantial debt to build infrastructure for OpenAI and other providers.

Strategic Position: Focused on specialized infrastructure for AI workloads, positioning as an alternative to major cloud providers for AI compute.

4. Growing Dependency on Subsidized AI

As AI capabilities become increasingly critical for businesses across sectors, a troubling pattern of dependency on heavily subsidized AI services is emerging. This dependency creates significant vulnerabilities for both startups and enterprises.

"The most successful AI vendors spend approximately $2.25 for every $1 they make - a fundamentally unsustainable business model that raises serious questions about the long-term viability of the current AI ecosystem."

— NoFriction Intelligence Report, "AI Cost Analysis 2025"

How Startups Are Becoming Trapped

AI startups face multiple dependency risks:

API Dependency

Many AI startups are building their entire business models on subsidized APIs from OpenAI, Anthropic, or Google. When subsidy economics shift, these startups face existential risk.

High Risk

Infrastructure Dependency

Many startups developing their own models rely on subsidized compute from major cloud providers like Microsoft Azure, who have their own competing AI offerings.

High Risk

Market Price Distortion

AI startups must compete with artificially low prices from subsidized competitors, forcing them to either operate at unsustainable losses or surrender market share.

Extreme Risk

Funding Asymmetry

Even well-funded startups with hundreds of millions in capital cannot match the billions being deployed by established tech giants in AI.

High Risk

"Even companies that raised hundreds of millions in funding are bowing out of the race to develop advanced AI models. While new funding for AI startups remains robust ($66.6 billion in Q1 2025 alone), many are discovering they cannot compete with Big Tech's massive AI investment."

— Bloomberg, "AI Startups Struggle to Keep Up With Big Tech's Spending Spree", 2024

Enterprise Dependency Vulnerabilities

Enterprises integrating AI into core business functions face different but equally serious risks:

Process Dependency

As enterprises build AI into critical workflows and business processes, switching costs increase dramatically, reducing negotiating leverage as subsidies inevitably decline.

Budget Planning Risk

Current AI pricing, artificially suppressed through subsidies, creates unrealistic expectations for long-term AI budget planning in enterprises.

Vendor Lock-in

Integration with specific AI platforms creates technical dependencies that become increasingly difficult to unwind as implementations scale.

Capability Withdrawal Risk

Features provided during the subsidy phase may be withdrawn or significantly altered as providers transition to sustainable economics.

IT Support Integration

5. Ethical and Governance Risks of AI Dependency

Beyond financial and operational concerns, dependency on subsidized AI presents serious ethical and governance challenges. As organizations increasingly rely on AI systems they neither own nor fully understand, complex questions arise about accountability, control, and long-term societal impacts.

Critical Ethical Concerns

Data Privacy & Sovereignty

Organizations feeding sensitive data into subsidized AI systems may have limited control over how this data is ultimately used for model training or analysis, creating privacy and competitive risks.

Accountability Diffusion

When AI systems cause harm or make errors, accountability becomes unclear in a subsidized ecosystem with multiple stakeholders (providers, developers, enterprises, end-users).

Algorithmic Control & Bias

Organizations have limited visibility into or control over how subsidized AI models may perpetuate biases or enforce particular worldviews that impact decisions and communications.

"The 'black box' nature of many AI algorithms obscures decision-making processes, making it difficult to assign responsibility and eroding consumer trust when things go wrong."

— AI Targeted Advertising Ethics Report, 2025

Governance Challenges in Subsidized AI

Effective AI governance becomes particularly difficult when organizations are dependent on subsidized external AI systems:

Market Power Concentration

The subsidy trap accelerates the concentration of AI power in the hands of a small number of tech giants, creating systemic risks:

  • Innovation Bottlenecks: Critical AI advancements increasingly controlled by a handful of companies
  • Digital Infrastructure Control: Small group of companies controlling essential digital infrastructure
  • Regulatory Capture: Concentrated AI market power potentially influencing regulatory directions
  • Value Extraction: Once dependencies are established, value can be extracted through pricing or terms changes

6. Signals of Coming Market Consolidation

Multiple indicators suggest that we are approaching the end of Phase 2 (Dominate) and entering Phase 3 (Consolidate) of the subsidy trap strategy. These signals point to a significant market restructuring on the horizon.

Early Consolidation Indicators

Talent Acquisition Without Full Company Purchase

Big Tech companies are increasingly "acqui-hiring" AI talent from startups without purchasing the entire company – a sign of value shifting from companies to key individuals.

"The sneaky way Big Tech is acquiring AI unicorns without actually acquiring them" — CNBC, August 2024

Emergence of "Zombiecorns"

AI companies valued at over $1 billion that cannot raise additional funding at previous valuations yet are too expensive to acquire outright – creating pressure for eventual down-round acquisitions.

"New report shows the staggering AI cash surge — and the rise of the 'zombiecorn'" — CNBC, May 2025

"Struggling AI Startups Look for a Bailout From Big Tech. Amazon, Google and Microsoft are using a new type of deal to get employees and technology from artificial-intelligence firms."

7. Strategic Recommendations by Stakeholder

Different stakeholders require tailored strategies to navigate the AI subsidy trap and coming market consolidation. Our recommendations are designed to help organizations protect their interests while preparing for the inevitable shift in market dynamics.

For AI Startups

Focus on Specialized Niches

Identify specialized vertical applications or industry-specific AI needs that are too niche for large players to address effectively. Build defensible positions in these areas with domain expertise and specialized datasets.

Develop Multi-Provider Flexibility

Architect solutions that can work with multiple underlying AI providers to reduce dependency on a single subsidized source. Create abstraction layers that allow for shifting between foundation models as economics change.

True Unit Economics Transparency

Establish clear understanding of true unit economics independent of current subsidized AI pricing. Build business models that can sustain realistic pricing after subsidies disappear, and communicate this sustainability to investors.

Strategic Positioning for Acquisition

If acquisition is the likely endgame, develop assets (talent, IP, customer relationships) that will be valuable to potential acquirers. Focus on complementary capabilities rather than direct competition with major players.

For Enterprise AI Adopters

Implement AI Vendor Diversification

Avoid single-provider dependency by implementing multi-vendor strategies for critical AI capabilities. Test deployments across different providers to understand migration challenges and establish alternatives.

Develop Realistic Total Cost Projections

Create financial models that account for significant price increases (3-5x current rates) for AI services over the next 24-36 months. Ensure budget planning accounts for the true economic cost of AI operations rather than temporary subsidized pricing.

Consider Strategic Insourcing

For mission-critical AI capabilities, evaluate opportunities to develop in-house expertise and leverage open-source models that can be operated independently of major providers, especially for sensitive or differentiated use cases.

Negotiate Data Rights & Price Protections

When entering agreements with AI providers, aggressively negotiate data usage rights, price increase caps, and transition provisions to protect against future market consolidation impacts. Consider multi-year price guarantees for critical services.

For Investors

Scrutinize Subsidy-Dependent Business Models

Apply rigorous scrutiny to business models built on subsidized AI services, testing viability under various pricing scenarios. Favor startups with clearly sustainable unit economics independent of current subsidies.

Position for Consolidation Opportunities

Identify potential acquisition targets and position early with strategic investments. Look for companies developing complementary technologies to major platforms rather than direct competitors.

Value Technical Differentiation

Prioritize investments in companies with genuine technical differentiation, proprietary data assets, or specialized domain expertise that creates barriers to competition from subsidized general-purpose AI.

Monitor Infrastructure Bottlenecks

Identify investment opportunities in AI infrastructure bottlenecks (specialized chips, energy-efficient computing, etc.) that may benefit regardless of which software players ultimately dominate.

For Policymakers

Evaluate Market Competition Impacts

Assess whether massive subsidization of AI services by dominant tech platforms constitutes anti-competitive behavior, particularly when these subsidies create dependencies that may lead to future market control.

Protect Critical Digital Infrastructure

Consider policies to ensure diverse, resilient AI infrastructure that isn't controlled by a small number of companies, potentially through support for open-source alternatives or public compute resources.

Address Data and Model Concentration

Develop frameworks for data portability, model access, and appropriate transparency to prevent excessive concentration of AI capabilities in a few companies.

Support Research Alternatives

Fund alternative research pathways and public infrastructure that could lead to more efficient, accessible AI technologies not dependent on massive subsidization from a few companies.

"Companies must approach AI with clear KPIs and measurement frameworks established before implementation to maximize return on investment."

— McKinsey & Deloitte Analysis, 2025

Sources: NoFriction Intelligence Analysis; Enterprise Agentic AI ROI Case Studies; McKinsey "State of AI" report 2025; Deloitte "AI Implementation Frameworks" 2025.

8. Conclusion: The Recarving of the Software Ecosystem

The artificial intelligence market is approaching a pivotal inflection point. After years of unprecedented subsidization by tech giants with seemingly unlimited resources, economic reality is beginning to assert itself. The "Great Subsidy Trap" we've analyzed throughout this report represents one of the most significant strategic market manipulations in technology history.

We are witnessing the early stages of a massive recarving of the entire software ecosystem. As AI becomes embedded in virtually every aspect of software, those who control AI capabilities will effectively control the broader software landscape.

The evidence is clear and compelling:

  • Economically Unsustainable: Current AI pricing bears no relation to actual costs. Leading AI providers are spending $2.25 for every $1 they earn, with projected losses in the billions.
  • Strategically Deliberate: These are not accidental losses but calculated investments in market dominance, following a clear pattern of subsidize, dominate, and consolidate.
  • Pervasive Dependencies: Both startups and enterprises are becoming increasingly dependent on subsidized AI services, creating vulnerabilities when subsidies inevitably end.
  • Consolidation Signals: Multiple indicators suggest we are entering the consolidation phase, with early signs visible in talent acquisition strategies and financial pressures on even the largest players.

The Coming Correction

Based on our analysis, we predict that the current AI market structure is not sustainable beyond 12-24 months. When the correction comes, it will likely take one of two forms:

Scenario: The Great Acquisition Wave

Major tech platforms will accelerate acquisition of AI startups, talent, and technologies as funding dries up and valuations adjust downward. This will further concentrate AI capabilities in a handful of companies, potentially raising antitrust concerns.

Scenario: The Pricing Reset

Leading AI providers will begin significant price increases toward economically sustainable levels, causing disruption for companies built on subsidized economics and triggering a wave of business model failures.

Most likely, we will see a combination of both scenarios playing out simultaneously across different segments of the market.

"The current trajectory of AI costs versus pricing is mathematically unsustainable. Either prices must rise dramatically or costs must fall by orders of magnitude. There is no third option. When this correction happens, it will reshape the entire AI landscape."

— Casey Potenzone, Founder, NoFriction

Preparing for the New Landscape

Organizations that recognize these dynamics and take proactive steps as outlined in our recommendations will be better positioned to navigate the coming market restructuring. Those who build their strategies on the assumption that current subsidized AI economics will continue indefinitely are at serious risk.

The winners in this new landscape will be:

  • For Enterprises: Those who develop flexibility in their AI strategy, realistic budget projections, and reduced dependency on any single provider.
  • For Startups: Those focused on sustainable unit economics, specialized niches, and clear differentiation that can't be easily replicated by general-purpose AI.
  • For Investors: Those who look beyond the AI hype to identify genuinely sustainable business models and position for the consolidation opportunity.

The AI revolution is real and transformative. The technology will continue to advance and create immense value. But the current market structure – built on billions in subsidies creating an artificial price environment – is not sustainable. A significant market correction is coming, and preparation is essential.

The coming years will witness a fundamental restructuring of who creates, controls, and captures value from artificial intelligence. Those who understand the "Great Subsidy Trap" and its inevitable conclusion will be best positioned to navigate what may be the most significant reorganization of the software industry in decades.
