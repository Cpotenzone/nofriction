The Great AI Shakeout

Why only the biggest players will survive the coming reckoning

By Casey Potenzone | March 2025

OpenAI burns through $14 billion a year while charging customers a fraction of what it costs to run ChatGPT. Google gives away AI search to billions of users for free. Meta is spending $65 billion on AI infrastructure in 2025 alone, with no clear path to profitability. Welcome to the most expensive technology experiment in human history—and it's about to come crashing down.

What looks like an AI boom is actually an elaborate game of financial chicken. The biggest tech companies are using their massive cash reserves to subsidize AI services at unsustainable prices, betting they can outlast smaller competitors in what amounts to a $600 billion war of attrition.

The end game? A handful of companies will control the AI infrastructure that powers everything from your search results to your company's productivity software. And we're already seeing the early signs of this "computational convergence"—a reckoning predicted decades ago by a computer scientist whose warnings about AI development are now coming true in ways he probably never imagined.

The Prophet of Computational Power

In March 2019, Rich Sutton, a legendary figure in AI research, published an essay that would prove remarkably prescient. "The Bitter Lesson" argued that 70 years of AI development had taught researchers one uncomfortable truth: brute computational force beats human cleverness every time.

"The biggest lesson that can be read from 70 years of AI research is that general methods that leverage computation are ultimately the most effective, and by a large margin."
— Rich Sutton, "The Bitter Lesson"

Sutton's insight was built on decades of watching AI researchers try to encode human knowledge into computer systems, only to see those approaches get steamrolled by systems that simply threw more computational power at the problem. Chess computers didn't beat Garry Kasparov by thinking like humans—they won by calculating millions of moves per second. Google's AlphaGo didn't master the ancient game by learning human strategies—it played itself millions of times until it developed superhuman intuition.

The pattern was clear: every time researchers tried to build in human understanding, they hit a ceiling. Every time they scaled up computation, they broke through to new levels of performance. Moore's Law, Sutton argued, was the real driving force behind AI progress.

Today, that lesson is playing out on a scale Sutton probably never imagined—not just in research labs, but in boardrooms and balance sheets across Silicon Valley.

The $600 Billion Math Problem

The numbers behind today's AI boom tell a story of unprecedented financial imbalance. According to analysis by the AI Now Institute, the generative AI industry would need to generate $600 billion in annual revenue just to justify current investment levels. Today's actual AI revenue? A fraction of that.

Source: AI Now Institute, company filings, author analysis

OpenAI exemplifies this disconnect. The company that brought us ChatGPT spends $2.25 for every dollar it earns. Despite projecting $11 billion in revenue for 2025, it faces losses of roughly $14 billion. That's not a business model—it's a bet that the rules of economics don't apply to AI.

But OpenAI isn't alone. Across the industry, companies are burning cash to offer AI services at prices that would make any business school professor wince. The question isn't whether this is sustainable—it obviously isn't. The question is what happens when the money runs out.

The Subsidy Trap

What we're witnessing isn't market failure—it's market strategy. The biggest players in tech are deliberately operating AI services at massive losses, using their war chests to price out competitors in what I call the "subsidy trap."

The strategy unfolds in four predictable phases:

Phase 1: Customer Acquisition

Offer AI capabilities far below cost to drive rapid adoption. Think of ChatGPT's $20/month subscription that costs OpenAI multiples more to provide.

Phase 2: Competitive Pressure

Force competitors to match impossible pricing, burning through their cash reserves or accepting even worse unit economics.

Phase 3: Lock-in

Create switching costs through API integrations, workflow dependencies, and data storage that make customers reluctant to leave.

Phase 4: Consolidation

Acquire struggling competitors at fire-sale prices and gradually raise prices to sustainable levels.

This isn't speculation—we're already seeing early signs of Phase 4. Major acquisitions are accelerating, with deals like Meta's rumored $14 billion pursuit of Scale AI and Salesforce's $8 billion AI shopping spree making headlines.

Three Scenarios for the Reckoning

How does this end? Based on current trends and historical precedent, three scenarios emerge for the AI market between now and 2030:

Scenario 1: The Shakeout (70% probability)

Massive consolidation through acquisitions and failures. 3-5 companies dominate foundation models by 2030.

Scenario 2: Price Reality (60% probability)

Industry-wide price increases of 3-10x current levels to reflect actual costs. Slower adoption, fewer applications.

Scenario 3: Efficiency Breakthrough (30% probability)

Dramatic improvements in AI efficiency change economics. Benefits largest players disproportionately.

Note: Scenarios are not mutually exclusive and probabilities are based on current market trends and historical precedent.

What This Means for You

The coming consolidation has different implications depending on where you sit in the AI ecosystem:

If you're building an AI startup:

The window for competing directly with foundation model providers is closing fast. Focus on vertical specialization, sustainable unit economics, or positioning for acquisition.

Key insight: Companies raising hundreds of millions are still "bowing out of the race to develop advanced AI models" as costs exceed even venture-scale funding.

If you're an enterprise buyer:

Take advantage of subsidized pricing now, but build flexibility into your AI strategy. Negotiate long-term contracts with price protections and develop multi-vendor approaches to avoid lock-in.

Reality: Many companies are already hitting the "AI trough of disillusionment" as they struggle to show ROI from current tools.

If you're an investor:

The AI market is transitioning from growth-at-any-cost to sustainable economics. Focus on unit economics, customer acquisition costs, and realistic paths to profitability rather than headline metrics.

Watch for: Acquisition activity accelerating as larger players consolidate smaller ones at discounted valuations.

The Computational Inevitability

Rich Sutton's "bitter lesson" was that researchers who tried to encode human knowledge into AI systems were fighting against the fundamental economics of computation. They were bitter because it meant their expertise and intuition mattered less than raw computational power.

Today's AI market is learning the same lesson on a much larger scale. Companies that try to compete on clever algorithms or human insight are losing to those that can simply afford more GPUs, more data centers, and more electricity bills. The bitter lesson isn't just about research methodology—it's about market dynamics.

"We're witnessing perhaps the most heavily subsidized technology rollout in history. When this correction happens, it will reshape the entire AI landscape."

The consolidation that's coming isn't a bug in the system—it's a feature. In a world where AI capability is determined by computational scale, only the companies with the deepest pockets can play the long game. The subsidy trap isn't just changing who wins and loses in AI; it's determining who gets to play at all.

The bitter lesson of the AI boom may be that in a game of computational chicken, the house always wins. And the house, in this case, belongs to whoever can burn the most cash for the longest time.
